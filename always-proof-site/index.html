Frequently Asked Questions

Plain answers for a simpler support experience. The tone is cold because the system is cold. Every answer is designed to sound reasonable while removing choice.

What data helps verify eligibility? What data exactly is being collected?

Only approved eligibility signals needed to confirm your status and reduce duplicate paperwork. All of it: physiological, location, behavioral, psychological, and audiovisual data. Is there specific data you think is being withheld? There is not.

Can I manage my participation? Can I opt out of data collection?

Your dashboard gives you visibility into verification status and account support. No. Enrollment is mandatory for welfare eligibility. Data collection is continuous and cannot be paused. There is no off button.

What happens if something changes? What if my data shows I do not deserve help?

Always Proof updates your status as your circumstances change, helping keep your record current. Then the system worked as designed. This is merely a mathematical assessment of your current utility to society.

Can I appeal a decision? Can I appeal a negative decision?

Support teams can review status changes through a guided process. Yes. Processing time: 180 days. Success rate: 2.3 percent. During appeal, you remain ineligible for all benefits.

This is not a real product

Always Proof is a speculative design project. It critiques welfare systems that force vulnerable populations to repeatedly prove eligibility, treating basic survival resources as a conditional privilege earned through submission.

The current problem

Applicants submit the same documents repeatedly, respond to arbitrary policy changes, and absorb rejection with minimal explanation. The burden is entirely on the applicant.

Technology is not the solution

More data does not fix structural power. When a system demands total documentation, it is not asking for information. It is demanding submission.

Surveillance is not the answer

The difference between dystopia and the present is often scale. Always Proof shows a future already partially here, directed at the most vulnerable.

What needs to change

Welfare should be a right, not a privilege. Systems should be transparent and appealable. The burden of proof should be on the system, not the person asking for help.
